Beginner

Start Here

The fundamentals every prompt engineer needs to know.

● Beginner

Be Specific, Not Vague

Replace generic words with precise descriptions. Instead of "write something good", say "write a 500-word persuasive essay arguing that remote work increases productivity, targeting skeptical managers".

● Beginner

Assign a Role

Start with "You are a [role]..." to prime the AI's perspective. "You are a senior UX designer with 10 years of experience" produces far better design advice than a generic prompt.

● Beginner

Define the Output Format

Tell the AI exactly how you want the response: "Format as a numbered list", "Use markdown headers", "Respond in JSON", or "Keep it under 200 words". Formatting instructions dramatically improve usability.

● Beginner

Provide Context

The more relevant context you give, the better the output. Include your audience, purpose, constraints, and any background information the AI needs to give you a tailored response.

● Beginner

Use Examples (Few-Shot)

Show the AI what you want by including 1-3 examples in your prompt. "Here's an example of the style I want: [example]. Now write a similar one about [topic]."

● Beginner

Iterate and Refine

Treat prompting as a conversation. If the first response isn't perfect, refine your prompt based on what was missing. Each iteration gets you closer to the ideal output.

Advanced

Advanced Techniques

Level up with professional prompt engineering strategies.

◆ Advanced

Chain-of-Thought Prompting

Add "Let's think step by step" or "Walk me through your reasoning" to get the AI to reason through complex problems methodically, dramatically improving accuracy on logic and math tasks.

◆ Advanced

Constraint Engineering

Add creative constraints to force more innovative outputs: "Explain this without using technical jargon", "Write this as a dialogue between two characters", or "Use only analogies from nature".

◆ Advanced

Persona Stacking

Combine multiple expert perspectives: "You are both a behavioral psychologist and a marketing expert. Analyze why [behavior] occurs and how to leverage it ethically in a product launch."

◆ Advanced

Negative Prompting

Explicitly state what you DON'T want: "Do not use bullet points", "Avoid clichés", "Don't include disclaimers". Negative constraints are just as powerful as positive instructions.

◆ Advanced

Temperature Control via Language

Use language to control creativity: "Be precise and factual" for analytical tasks vs. "Be imaginative and unconventional" for creative work. This guides the AI's output style effectively.

◆ Advanced

Prompt Chaining

Break complex tasks into a sequence of prompts where each output feeds the next. This is more reliable than one massive prompt and gives you control at each step of the process.

Examples

Good vs Bad Prompts

See the difference specificity makes in real examples.

✗ Bad Prompt

"Write something about marketing."

✓ Good Prompt

"You are a B2B marketing expert. Write a 600-word LinkedIn article about how small SaaS companies can use content marketing to reduce CAC by 30%. Include 3 actionable strategies with real examples. Tone: authoritative but approachable."

✗ Bad Prompt

"Fix my code."

✓ Good Prompt

"Review this Python 3.11 function that processes user authentication. Identify bugs, security vulnerabilities (especially SQL injection risks), and performance bottlenecks. Provide a refactored version with inline comments explaining each change."

✗ Bad Prompt

"Make a logo."

✓ Good Prompt

"Design a minimalist logo concept for a fintech startup called 'Vaultr' that targets millennials. The brand values are: trust, speed, and innovation. Use geometric shapes, a blue-gold color palette, and ensure it works at both large and favicon sizes."
